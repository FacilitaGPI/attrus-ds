Design system

Graphic elements

Two support assets beyond the logo and symbol — the rounded triangle (apex glyph extracted from the "A") and the dot pattern (a network matrix). Both add atmosphere and depth without competing with content.

01Rounded triangle

Form derived from the logo's "A". A softly rounded equilateral triangle that points upward — read variously as an arrow, a mountain, a convergence point. Applied to covers, dividers, backgrounds and patterns.

  1. Construction Equilateral triangle with rounded corners (radius ≈ 6% of the side). The apex sits at the top — never inverted. Matches the proportions of the apex inside the wordmark.
  2. Source SVG inline (#apex-glyph symbol). Renders crisply at any size; fills via currentColor so it inherits the parent's text colour.
  3. Role Always a support element — never replaces the logo, text or photography. Adds atmosphere; carries no information of its own.
  4. Limit Maximum 2 instances per piece. More than that and the shape stops being supportive — it becomes the subject.

06Don'ts

Six anti-patterns. Each one turns a support element into a focal element — exactly what these tokens are not.

Don't invert

The triangle always points upward. Inverting destroys the "arrow / convergence" reading and turns the mark into a generic warning glyph.

Don't rotate or tilt

The triangle is always axis-aligned. Tilting feels arbitrary and breaks the structural metaphor.

Don't use off-brand colours

Stay within the official palette — primary, secondary, accent, neutral, or white-alpha overlays on dark. Never feedback colours (success / warning / danger) — those carry meaning.

Don't stack more than two

The brand book caps at 2 instances per piece. Beyond that the triangle stops being supportive and starts feeling like decoration.

Don't use the dot pattern at 100%

The dot pattern is atmospheric, not a focal layer. Anything above 22% opacity turns the surface into a graph paper that fights copy.
