Design system

Composition & hierarchy

How to arrange a screen so it reads itself. Four tools — type, proximity, enclosure and rhythm — turn a pile of data into a structure the eye can scan in order. The platform is dense; these rules are what keep dense from becoming cluttered.

01Composition is reading order

Before colour or polish, a screen has one job: tell the eye where to start, what's grouped, and what's secondary. Every technique here serves that — establishing a hierarchy (what matters most) and clusters (what belongs together). Four tools, in order of cost:

01 · Type
Size, weight & colour rank importance. The cheapest hierarchy — costs no space.
02 · Proximity
Whitespace groups. Near = related, far = separate. No borders needed.
03 · Enclosure
Cards, dividers & section heads when proximity alone isn't enough.
04 · Rhythm
Consistent spacing makes the structure predictable and calm.

02Hierarchy by type

The type scale isn't decoration — each step is a rank. Pair size with weight and a foreground role (default → secondary → muted) so three levels of importance are legible at a glance, before the reader has read a single word.

The rule

Use at most three type levels per cluster: a label, a value, a caption. More than three and the ranking blurs.

Carry rank with weight and colour, not just size — a 14px bold default-foreground line outranks a 14px regular muted line on the same row.

03Grouping by proximity

Whitespace is the cheapest grouping tool in the system — it needs no borders, no fills, no colour. The Gestalt principle: items placed close together read as one group; a larger gap reads as a separation. Vary the gap between clusters vs within them and structure appears for free.

The rule

Make the gap between groups at least 2× the gap within a group. If a label and its value are 4px apart, the next field should be 12–16px away.

Reach for a divider or a card only after proximity isn't enough — usually it is.

04Enclosure & shapes

When proximity can't carry the grouping — because clusters sit side by side, or a region needs to feel "contained" — reach for shapes: the card, the divider, and the section header. Each is a stronger, more expensive signal than whitespace, so use the lightest one that works.

Escalating strength

Whitespace — default. Groups without drawing a single line.

Divider — a 1px rule when two groups share a surface but must read as distinct.

Card — a bordered surface when a cluster is an object you act on (a row, a metric, a form).

Section header — an eyebrow or H-level title when a region needs a name.

Section header · names a region

Two cards = two objects, side by side. The header groups them under one name.

A divider separates without enclosing — lighter than a card.

Over-enclosed — boxes in boxes

Nested borders add visual noise and steal horizontal space for nothing.

One card, rows split by hairlines

One enclosure for the group; hairlines for the items inside it.

05Density & rhythm

A dense operational platform lives or dies on consistent spacing. Every gap should come from the spacing scale — never an arbitrary number. Predictable spacing is what makes a data-heavy screen feel calm instead of cramped.

The rule

Pick a base rhythm for the screen (most platform surfaces use --space-5/16px between clusters, --space-4/12px inside).

Keep one rhythm per region. Mixing 11px, 13px, 19px gaps reads as misalignment even when nothing is broken.

Density is a choice: tighten for tables and scanning, loosen for marketing and onboarding.

08Emphasis & inverse surfaces

Two more levers for hierarchy: emphasis — spending the Sky accent on the single thing that matters — and inverse surfaces, where the same composition rules hold but the colour roles flip. (Full colour rules live in Accent & highlights.)

Emphasis = one focal point

Hierarchy isn't only size and position — a single accent creates an instant entry point. Spend it on the row, value or action that matters most.

The discipline is one accent per view. Highlight everything and you've highlighted nothing.

Emphasis in running text

To pull one phrase out of a paragraph, lift it with the accent — either as coloured, semibold text or a soft highlight mark. Both flag the part that matters while keeping the sentence readable.

Emphasise one phrase per block. The moment two spans are highlighted in the same paragraph, neither wins — the same 3% discipline as colour.

Technique A · accent text

A generic introductory sentence sets up the idea without urgency. This is the phrase that carries the point, set in accent-700 and semibold so the eye catches it mid-paragraph — then the sentence continues in normal body weight.

Technique B · highlight mark

Generic supporting copy continues the thought, and when a span deserves the strongest pull, a soft highlight mark wraps it like a marker stroke. Reserve the mark for the single most important span.

Don't · over-highlight

When every other phrase is emphasised, the paragraph turns noisy and nothing stands out — emphasis spent everywhere is emphasis spent nowhere.

Inverse surfaces

On dark (inverse) surfaces — heroes, premium panels, decks — the structure is identical: same type ranks, same proximity, same rhythm.

Only the colour roles flip to the inverse tokens: text → --color-foreground-inverse-*, hairlines → --color-border-inverse, chips → white-alpha overlays.

Accent flips too: on dark, Sky-500 is legible (--color-foreground-inverse-accent) — the opposite of light, where Sky text must darken to accent-700.

09Checklist

A fast pass before shipping a screen. If any "don't" is true, the composition is fighting the reader.

Do establish one clear entry point — the single most important thing reads first.

Don't give five elements equal weight; the eye then has no place to land.

Do group with whitespace first — bigger gaps between clusters than within.

Don't reach for a border when a gap would group the items just as well.

Do cap each cluster at ~3 type levels: label, value, caption.

Don't nest cards inside cards — one enclosure per group, hairlines inside.

Do pull every gap from the spacing scale; keep one rhythm per region.

Don't spend the Sky accent on more than one focal point per view.
