Design system

Glass layers

Translucent surfaces with a backdrop blur. Used for floating chrome over imagery or gradient surfaces — toolbars, navigation bars, dropdowns, modals — when the underlying content should remain visible but visually quieted.

01Anatomy

A glass surface is the composition of four things — never just a translucent fill. Skip any of them and the effect collapses into a flat washed-out shape.

Floating toolbar
Hovers above scrolling content with a subtle frosted plate
  1. Translucent fill A glass-tinted background (--glass-white or --glass-ink) layered over the underlying surface — alpha mixed with color-mix() against transparent.
  2. Backdrop blur A backdrop-filter token (--glass-blur-sm/md/lg) that blurs everything behind the element, not the element itself. Without blur the panel reads as a flat colored shape.
  3. Hairline border A 1px line (--glass-border on light, --glass-border-dark on dark) that seats the panel against any background — light or busy. Skip it and the edges dissolve.
  4. Spec-glass shadow Inset top-highlight + outer drop (--shadow-glass / --shadow-glass-dark). Simulates the spec highlight on a real piece of glass.

02Tints & intensities

Seven tokenised glass tints — three light intensities, three dark intensities, plus a bluish "system tint". Pick by surface contrast: light glass on dark bg, dark glass on light bg.

Light glass --glass-white-lo / -white / -white-hi

Three intensities of white-tinted fill. Use -lo when only a hint of frost is wanted; -hi when opacity is needed for readability without losing the glass character.

on dark bg
--glass-white-lo
38% white. Hint of frost. For overlays where readability is provided by adjacent contrast.
--glass-white
58% white. Default light glass. Use for most floating chrome over imagery.
--glass-white-hi
78% white. Near-opaque. Use when copy must stay readable over a busy backdrop.

Dark glass --glass-ink-lo / -ink / -ink-hi

Same intensities, navy-tinted. Used on dark heroes, marketing pages, or inverse-layer surfaces. Pair with --glass-border-dark and --shadow-glass-dark.

on dark bg
--glass-ink-lo
35% navy. Subtle inverse frost. Use when ink should barely tint the background.
--glass-ink
55% navy. Default dark glass. Use for nav over imagery, dark modals over dark heroes.
--glass-ink-hi
78% navy. Near-opaque. Use when the panel must dominate without reading as a solid surface.

System tint --glass-tint

Bluish-white tint that picks up the brand colour through the blur — feels like ATTRUS branded glass. Use for product chrome that wants to declare "this is part of the platform".

brand variant
--glass-tint
55% neutral-50 (bluish white). Picks up the underlying brand colour without being heavy-handed.

03Blur strengths

Three tokenised blur levels. Choose by how much the user needs to see the content behind the glass — and how much breathing room the panel itself has.

--glass-blur-sm
10px blur
Light frost. Use when the background is decorative and the panel sits ≤44px tall (toolbars, breadcrumbs, sticky bars).
--glass-blur
18px blur · default
Medium frost. Use for standard floating chrome — dropdowns, popovers, command palettes. Tweak only when you have a reason.
--glass-blur-lg
28px blur
Heavy frost. Use for blocking surfaces — modals over busy heroes, full-screen sheets where the underlying content must recede.

05Shadow & border

Glass surfaces use a paired shadow + border treatment. The border seats the panel; the shadow gives it physical depth and an inset highlight that simulates real glass.

On light backgrounds

Pair --glass-border (1px white at 45%) with --shadow-glass (inset top highlight + outer blue drop). The inset is what makes glass read as glass and not as a flat colored shape.

light variant
Pure glass plate
Notice the top edge is brighter than the bottom — the inset 1px white highlight at 50% opacity, the spec highlight of real glass.

On dark backgrounds

Pair --glass-border-dark (1px white at 10%) with --shadow-glass-dark (inset 8% white highlight + outer black drop). Without the inset highlight, dark glass dissolves into the surface beneath.

dark variant
Dark glass plate
Lower-opacity inset (8% vs 50%) — just enough to seat the top edge against the dark background.

06Accessibility

Glass surfaces are tempting but trade contrast for atmosphere. Treat them as decoration first, container second.

ConcernRuleWhy
Body text contrast Never put long-form reading copy on glass. Backdrop blur cannot guarantee the 4.5:1 contrast required by WCAG AA — the underlying surface can shift it at any time.
Short labels OK on -hi intensities only. Nav links, button labels, headers (≥ --fs-md + --w-semibold) can sit on glass-white-hi or glass-ink-hi where the fill is near-opaque.
Focus rings Always use --shadow-focus on interactive children, never rely on the glass border. The 1px hairline border is too soft for keyboard focus visibility.
Reduced motion backdrop-filter is static — no motion concerns. Animations on the surface itself should respect prefers-reduced-motion.
Fallback Browsers without backdrop-filter support fall back to the translucent fill alone. Test on older Safari / Firefox versions — the fill stays readable, but the "glass" effect disappears gracefully.

07Don'ts

Glass is a finishing touch, not a base material. These are the five ways teams reach for it and shouldn't.

No blur · flat
Just a translucent fill — the panel reads as washed out, not as glass.

Don't skip the blur. Without backdrop-filter, the panel is a flat tinted shape — not glass. Always include one of the --glass-blur-* tokens.

Over plain white
Glass on a flat surface has nothing to blur.

Don't put glass over flat surfaces. If the background is a solid token (canvas, surface, subtle), use a regular card. Glass needs imagery, gradient or photographic noise behind it to do its job.

Eligibility & thresholds

Don't put body copy on glass. Reading paragraphs on translucent surfaces fails WCAG AA contrast in many backgrounds. Reserve glass for chrome — toolbars, navs, popovers — not content surfaces.

Custom 40px blur
Off-token. The system has 3 intensities for a reason.

Don't invent blur values. Use --glass-blur-sm / --glass-blur / --glass-blur-lg. Custom values fragment the visual rhythm and break consistency across surfaces.

No border, no shadow
Edges dissolve. The panel reads as a smudge, not a piece of glass.

Don't drop the border or shadow. A glass surface needs the hairline border AND the spec-shadow inset highlight to be perceived as a physical pane. Without them it's a smudge.
